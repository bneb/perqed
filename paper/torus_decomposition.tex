\begin{abstract}
We present machine-checked formal proofs of the $m=4$ and $m=6$ base cases
of the Directed Hamiltonian Torus Decomposition problem, verified by the
Lean~4 kernel via the \texttt{decide} tactic. While an elegant general
construction for even $m \ge 8$ was recently discovered by Aquino-Michaels,
the small even cases remained open. We use Simulated Annealing with a
non-monotonic cooling schedule to find valid decompositions for the
$4 \times 4 \times 4$ directed torus (64 vertices) and the
$6 \times 6 \times 6$ directed torus (216 vertices, 648 arcs), finding the
latter in under 100 seconds on an Apple~M4 processor. We then verify both
results inside Lean's trusted kernel. We describe the energy function, the
avoidance of functional graph traps, and the computational scaling of the
\texttt{decide} tactic to the 216-vertex topology.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Consider a three-dimensional directed graph $G_m$ with vertex set
$V = \{(i, j, k) : 0 \le i, j, k < m\}$ and directed edges from each vertex
$(i,j,k)$ to $(i{+}1, j, k)$, $(i, j{+}1, k)$, and $(i, j, k{+}1)$, all
modulo~$m$. This graph has $m^3$ vertices and $3m^3$ arcs.

\begin{definition}
A \emph{Directed Hamiltonian Torus Decomposition} of $G_m$ is a partition of
the $3m^3$ arcs into exactly 3 directed Hamiltonian cycles, each of
length~$m^3$.
\end{definition}

Knuth posed this problem in the context of Volume~4B of \emph{The Art of
Computer Programming}~\cite{knuth2026}. In February 2026, Anthropic's Claude
Opus~4.6 found a generalized algebraic construction for all odd~$m$, which
Knuth verified and proved correct. During the same exploration, Claude found
isolated empirical solutions for $m = 4$, $m = 6$, and $m = 8$, but could
not generalize them. In a March~6 revision, Knuth noted that Keston
Aquino-Michaels found an elegant decomposition for even~$m$, though the
construction relies on a splice gadget that strictly requires $m \ge 8$.
The small even cases $m = 4$ and $m = 6$ fall outside the scope of that
general proof and remained unverified.

Morrison formalized Knuth's odd-$m$ proof in Lean~4 on March~4, 2026.

We make the following contributions to close the remaining gap:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Machine-checked proofs of the $m = 4$ and $m = 6$ decompositions,
  stated as pure \texttt{Prop}-valued theorems in Lean~4 and verified
  inside the trusted kernel without external SAT/SMT solvers.
  \item A stochastic search architecture that successfully navigates the
  $6^{216}$ state space of the $m=6$ torus using a corrected in-degree
  energy function and non-monotonic cooling.
  \item A non-monotonic cooling schedule with dynamic threshold-based
  reheating~\cite{kirkpatrick1983,marinari1992}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}

\subsection{State Space and Scaling}

At each vertex, the three outgoing arcs are assigned to three colors
$\{0, 1, 2\}$. There are $3! = 6$ valid assignments per vertex, giving a
search space of $6^{64} \approx 6.3 \times 10^{49}$ for $m=4$.

For $m=6$, the graph grows to 216 vertices and 648 arcs. The state space
explodes to $6^{216} \approx 1.05 \times 10^{168}$. Pinning $\sigma[0] = 0$
reduces this by a factor of 6, but the sheer scale renders standard exact
formulations highly memory-intensive.

We represent a state as an array $\sigma \in \{0, \ldots, 5\}^{m^3}$, where
$\sigma[v]$ selects which permutation of $\{0,1,2\}$ is applied to the
outgoing directions $(+x, +y, +z)$ at vertex~$v$.

\subsection{Energy Function}

Each color subgraph has out-degree~1 at every vertex by construction. However,
the in-degree is not guaranteed to be~1: a vertex may receive zero or multiple
incoming arcs of a given color from its three axial predecessors. A graph with
out-degree~1 but variable in-degree is a functional graph, whose components
consist of directed cycles with trees feeding into them. A naive cycle-counting
energy function can assign $E = 0$ to such a lasso topology, incorrectly
reporting it as a Hamiltonian cycle.

\begin{definition}
Let $C_c(\sigma)$ be the number of components traced by following successors
in the color-$c$ subgraph, and let $D_c(\sigma)$ be the number of vertices
with in-degree $\ne 1$ for color~$c$. The energy is
\[
  E(\sigma) = \sum_{c=0}^{2} \bigl(C_c(\sigma) - 1\bigr)
            + \sum_{c=0}^{2} D_c(\sigma).
\]
\end{definition}

The in-degree penalty ensures that $E = 0$ requires each color subgraph to
be a single cycle visiting all $m^3$ vertices, i.e., a Hamiltonian cycle.
The energy can be computed in $O(m^3)$ time. For the $m=6$ engine, we
implemented an incremental energy evaluator using in-place mutation with
rollback: the in-degree delta is computed in $O(1)$ per color and the cycle
count is recalculated only for colors whose successor changed. This yielded
a 12$\times$ throughput improvement over the immutable full-recalculation
baseline, verified by a 300{,}000-mutation fuzz test asserting exact
agreement between incremental and full energy at every step.

\subsection{Non-Monotonic Cooling}

We use the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm with a single-vertex mutation
operator. A random vertex $v \in \{1, \ldots, m^3{-}1\}$ is selected and its
permutation changed to one of the five alternatives.

The cooling schedule is $T_k = T_0 \cdot \alpha^k$ with $T_0 = 50$ and
$\alpha = 0.999998$. When the best energy has not improved for $W$ iterations,
the temperature is set to $T_r = \max(1,\; E_{\text{best}}^{2/5})$ and the
window is doubled ($W \leftarrow 2W$). Upon improvement, $W$ resets. This
non-monotonic schedule allows the search to escape deep local
minima~\cite{kirkpatrick1983,marinari1992}.

Notably, the same cooling rate and reheat formula were used for both $m=4$
and $m=6$ without modification. The only change for $m=6$ was the use of
the incremental energy evaluator to increase iteration throughput.

\subsection{Comparison with Exact Solvers}

For $m = 4$, the decomposition problem is small enough that exact solvers
are practical. An ILP encoding using Miller-Tucker-Zemlin sub-tour
elimination~\cite{miller1960}, or a pure Boolean SAT encoding using
positional reachability constraints, would solve this instance in
milliseconds. Our stochastic approach is not competitive with exact solvers
at this scale.

However, the SA pipeline evaluates Hamiltonicity in $O(V)$ time with $V$
bytes of working memory. At $m=6$ (216 vertices, $6^{216} \approx 10^{168}$
states), this memory efficiency becomes relevant: the SA engine successfully
found the decomposition in under 100 seconds with negligible memory overhead.

\section{Formal Verification and Kernel Scaling}

The discovered witnesses are arrays in $\{0,\ldots,5\}^{m^3}$,
each selecting a permutation of the three outgoing directions at a vertex.
We decode each array into three successor functions $f_0$, $f_1$, $f_2$,
each mapping $\mathbb{Z}_m^3 \to \mathbb{Z}_m^3$.

The mathematical specification is stated entirely in Lean's \texttt{Prop}
universe. We define the torus edge relation directly on
$\texttt{Fin}\;m \times \texttt{Fin}\;m \times \texttt{Fin}\;m$, using
the built-in modular arithmetic on \texttt{Fin}:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=ML,mathescape=true]
def isTorusEdge (u v : Torus6) : Prop :=
  v = (u.1 + 1, u.2.1, u.2.2) $\lor$
  v = (u.1, u.2.1 + 1, u.2.2) $\lor$
  v = (u.1, u.2.1, u.2.2 + 1)
\end{lstlisting}
We deliberately use \texttt{Fin\;m} rather than Mathlib's \texttt{ZMod\;m}
to minimize the trusted computing base. In Lean~4's foundation, addition
on $\texttt{Fin}\;n$ is defined as $(a + b) \mod n$, which is
definitionally identical to the \texttt{ZMod}\;$n$ ring structure for
concrete~$n$. This identity is witnessed by Mathlib's
\texttt{finEquivZMod}, which lifts trivially to the 3D product.
Our formalization therefore verifies the standard mathematical object
$(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})^3$ without incurring a Mathlib dependency.

Hamiltonicity is defined via finite iteration rather than abstract
reachability. A function $f$ on $m^3$ vertices is a Hamiltonian cycle iff it
is injective, its orbit from $(0,0,0)$ closes at exactly $m^3$ steps, and no
shorter orbit exists. The decomposition theorem requires all three
functions to produce valid torus edges, to be pairwise disjoint, and to be
Hamiltonian cycles.

The proof for each value of~$m$ is decomposed into 15 sub-theorems, each
verified by the \texttt{decide} tactic inside Lean's kernel:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=ML,mathescape=true]
theorem torus_m6_valid :
    IsHamiltonianDecomposition f0 f1 f2 :=
  $\langle$edges_f0, edges_f1, edges_f2,
   disj_01, disj_02, disj_12,
   $\langle$inj_f0, orbit_f0, no_short_f0$\rangle$,
   $\langle$inj_f1, orbit_f1, no_short_f1$\rangle$,
   $\langle$inj_f2, orbit_f2, no_short_f2$\rangle\rangle$
\end{lstlisting}

Scaling the verification to $m=6$ presented a stress test for Lean's
trusted kernel. The proof of injectivity requires evaluating
$216^2 = 46{,}656$ pairwise comparisons, while the cycle validation
requires verifying three 216-step orbits and $3 \times 215 = 645$
sub-tour exclusions. Despite the combinatorial explosion, Lean's
\texttt{decide} tactic successfully reduced all \texttt{Prop}-valued
sub-theorems without requiring boolean reflection or custom C compilation
(i.e., \texttt{native\_decide}). Both the $m=4$ and $m=6$ proofs
compile cleanly with zero axioms and no unproven subgoals.

The trusted computing base consists solely of Lean's kernel type checker.
Unlike \texttt{native\_decide}, which compiles terms to C and trusts the
system compiler, \texttt{decide} performs all evaluation within the kernel
itself.

\section{Conclusion}

We have given machine-checked, \texttt{Prop}-valued proofs that both the
$4 \times 4 \times 4$ and $6 \times 6 \times 6$ directed tori admit
decompositions into three Hamiltonian cycles. Combined with the
Aquino-Michaels construction for even $m \ge 8$ and Knuth's construction
for odd~$m$, this computationally closes the directed Hamiltonian torus
decomposition problem for all~$m$.

All source code, the TypeScript stochastic engine, the incremental
energy evaluator, the witness payloads, and the Lean~4 proofs are
available at \url{https://github.com/bneb/perqed}.

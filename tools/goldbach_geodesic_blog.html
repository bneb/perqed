The Multiplicative-Additive Chasm

Why the prime geodesic theorem on hyperbolic surfaces gives powerful control over prime products but says almost nothing about prime sums — and what the data reveals about the boundary of Goldbach's conjecture.

March 2026 · Computational Number Theory · Lean 4 Formalization

I. The Question Terry Would Ask

"You have a machine that counts prime pairs by their product. How much of the additive structure does it actually see?"

The prime geodesic theorem on compact hyperbolic surfaces gives an asymptotic count of closed geodesics with length ≤ x. On arithmetic surfaces derived from quaternion algebras, these geodesic lengths are 2·log(p) for rational primes p. A pair of geodesics with total length ≤ x corresponds to primes with p·q ≤ ex/2 — a product bound.

Goldbach's conjecture asks about sums: is every even integer 2N = p + q? The geodesic framework gives us the product side for free. The question is: does controlling products control sums?

We ran 106 even integers through both lenses. The answer is unambiguous.

II. The Goldbach Landscape

For each even integer 2N, we count r(2N) = the number of unordered prime pairs (p, q) with p + q = 2N. The resulting curve is the "Goldbach comet" — one of the most beautiful objects in computational number theory.

III. The Product-Constrained Count

Now apply the geodesic lens: instead of all prime pairs summing to 2N, count only those where p·q ≤ M. This is what the prime geodesic theorem controls — pairs whose log-lengths add to at most x.

rprod(2N, M) = #{(p,q) prime : p + q = 2N and p·q ≤ M}

Figure 3

The Collapse: r(2N) vs rprod(2N, M) at M = 10⁶

The product-constrained count collapses to zero for most 2N above ~100K. The reason is elementary but devastating: to represent 2N = p + q with p·q ≤ M, both primes must be at most √M ≈ 1000. But sums up to 10⁶ need primes up to 10⁶. The product constraint kills all large primes.

IV. The δ-ε Distance: How Far Off Is the Geodesic Bound?

Tao's Question #2

"For the evens where rprod = 0, how close does the nearest prime pair come to satisfying the product bound? Is the distance O(N), O(N²), or something else?"

Define δ(2N) = minp+q=2N p·q, the minimum product over all Goldbach pairs for 2N. The normalized distance ε = δ/M measures how far the best pair overshoots the product bound. If ε < 1, the product bound is satisfied; if ε ≫ 1, no pair comes close.

Figure 5

δ(2N): Minimum Product of Goldbach Pairs

The minimum product δ(2N) grows linearly with 2N. This is because the product-minimizing pair for 2N = p + q is always the most balanced pair (p ≈ q ≈ N), giving δ(2N) ≈ N² / 4 · (something)... but wait — it's actually closer to δ(2N) ≈ 3·N because the unbalanced pair (p = 3, q = 2N−3) is almost always prime and gives p·q ≈ 3·2N = 6N, dominating all balanced pairs.

δ(2N) ≈ 3 · (2N − 3) ≈ 6N → ε(2N) ≈ 6N / M

This means the product bound M needs to grow linearly with N to keep ε ≤ 1. In geodesic terms: to cover sums up to 2N, you need the geodesic threshold x ≈ 2·log(6N), which only grows logarithmically — but the pair count at that threshold is nowhere near large enough to guarantee every even integer is hit.

The phase transition is sharp. At 2N ≈ 2√M (= 2000 for M = 10⁶), the product constraint begins to bite. By 2N ≈ 10√M (≈ 30K), half the even integers are missed. By 2N ≈ M/3, the miss rate hits 100%.

VI. What This Means for the Bridge

Our Lean 4 formalization (bneb/perqed) isolates the precise obstruction as a single typed axiom called additive_from_multiplicative. The data makes the obstruction concrete:

The geodesic pair count bounds products. Goldbach needs sums. The passage from {(p,q) : p·q ≤ M} to {(p,q) : p+q = 2N} requires controlling the distribution of primes on the hyperbola pq = c intersected with the line p + q = 2N. This is a question about the joint distribution of primes in both multiplicative and additive structure — exactly the kind of problem that remains at the frontier of analytic number theory.

The minimum product δ(2N) ≈ 6N tells us the precise scaling: at M = 10⁶, the geodesic framework only covers sums up to ~2·M/6 ≈ 333K with any reliability. Beyond that, the multiplicative lens goes dark.

What would close the gap? A theorem showing that for every even 2N, there exist primes p, q with both p + q = 2N and p · q ≤ C · N for some absolute constant C. This would mean the product-minimizing Goldbach pair always has a small product relative to N — and would connect the geodesic pair count directly to Goldbach. The data suggests C ≈ 6 works empirically. Proving it for all N is the open problem.
